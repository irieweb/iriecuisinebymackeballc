Food Stories

Every dish on our menu has a story. Explore the vibrant Caribbean roots behind the flavors you love at Irie Cuisine.

Jerk Chicken

Jerk chicken is the crown jewel of Jamaican cooking. The word "jerk" refers to both the spice blend and the cooking technique — meat is rubbed with a fiery mix of spices and slow-cooked over pimento wood for that unmistakable smoky, aromatic flavor.

The technique has roots in Jamaica's mountains, where the indigenous Taíno people developed methods of smoking meat over open fires. Over centuries, this evolved into the jerk style we know today, blending Taíno smoking methods with African seasoning traditions and Caribbean ingredients.

The signature marinade features scotch bonnet peppers, allspice berries (called pimento in Jamaica), thyme, garlic, ginger, and scallions. At Irie Cuisine, our jerk chicken is marinated for hours and slow-smoked over real charcoal.

🌶️ Fun Fact: Allspice is called "pimento" in Jamaica and grows natively on the island. It's the only major spice indigenous to the Western Hemisphere!
🍖

Oxtails

Jamaican oxtail is a beloved comfort food and Caribbean icon. Oxtail is the tail of cattle, cut into thick rounds with a central bone surrounded by rich meat and connective tissue. Cooked low and slow, the collagen melts into the braising liquid, creating a thick, glossy, incredibly flavorful gravy.

The oxtail is marinated with thyme, scallions, garlic, allspice, and browning sauce, then seared and braised for hours with butter beans, scotch bonnet pepper, and vegetables. Today it's one of the most sought-after meats in Caribbean cuisine worldwide.

🦴 Fun Fact: Oxtail is one of the most collagen-rich cuts of beef — great for joints and skin, and it creates that signature velvety gravy no other cut can match!
🐟

Escovitch Fish

Escovitch fish traces back to the Spanish "escabeche" — a technique of preserving fish in vinegar and spices brought to Jamaica in the 1500s. The word goes even further back to the Arabic "sikbaj" and Persian words for "vinegar" and "broth."

Jamaican cooks made it their own — adding scotch bonnet peppers, pimento berries, onions, carrots, and bell peppers. The fish (traditionally whole red snapper) is fried golden and crispy, then topped with the spicy, tangy pickled vegetables. Originally the vinegar kept fish fresh before refrigeration existed.

🌍 Fun Fact: The escovitch technique traveled from Persia to Spain to North Africa to Jamaica — each culture adding its own twist. The Jamaican version is considered the boldest of them all!

Curried Goat

Curried goat sits at the heart of Jamaican celebrations — weddings, parties, holidays, and Sunday dinners. The curry tradition came from Indian workers who arrived in the 1800s with their spice blends. Jamaicans fused curry with scotch bonnet pepper, allspice, thyme, and garlic to create something uniquely Caribbean.

The key is time — meat marinated overnight, then slowly braised until fork-tender. At Irie Cuisine, we serve ours with white rice and steamed vegetables.

🎉 Fun Fact: In Jamaica, curried goat is the ultimate party dish. If there's no curried goat, some guests might wonder if it's really a party!
🍲

Stew Chicken

Jamaican stew chicken is the ultimate comfort food. Bone-in chicken is marinated in thyme, allspice, garlic, and browning sauce, then caramelized in a hot pot before simmering with onions, bell peppers, carrots, and tomatoes.

The browning process — caramelizing sugar before adding the chicken — creates a depth of color and flavor unique to Caribbean cooking.

🥘 Fun Fact: The "browning" technique gives the dish its signature mahogany color and slightly sweet, complex flavor that you won't find anywhere else.
🥟

Beef Patties

The Jamaican beef patty is one of the most iconic Caribbean snacks — a flaky, golden pastry filled with spiced ground beef. Inspired by British Cornish pasties, Jamaican bakers added turmeric for that signature yellow crust and filled it with scotch bonnet pepper, thyme, allspice, and scallions.

💛 Fun Fact: The bright yellow color comes from turmeric in the dough — it adds a subtle earthy flavor that makes the crust special!

Rice & Peas

Rice and peas is Jamaica's signature side dish — the "peas" are actually red kidney beans! The rice is cooked in coconut milk with kidney beans, garlic, thyme, scallions, and scotch bonnet pepper, giving it a creamy richness that pairs perfectly with spicy mains.

This dish blends African and Asian cooking traditions that came together in Jamaica. At Irie Cuisine, our rice and peas is made fresh daily with real coconut milk.

🥥 Fun Fact: In Jamaica, Sunday dinner isn't complete without rice and peas. Some people call it "Sunday rice" — even though it tastes amazing any day of the week!
🍚

White Rice

Simple, fluffy white rice is a staple across the Caribbean and the perfect base for soaking up rich, flavorful sauces. In Jamaican cooking, white rice is the go-to side for dishes with heavy gravies like curried goat and oxtails — it lets the bold flavors of the main dish shine without competing.

Jamaican-style white rice is typically cooked with a touch of butter and salt, steamed until each grain is light and separate. It's the clean canvas that makes those saucy, spicy dishes complete.

🍛 Fun Fact: While rice and peas gets all the glory, white rice is the unsung hero of the Jamaican plate — it's the best way to catch every last drop of oxtail gravy!
🥬

Steamed Cabbage

Jamaican steamed cabbage is nothing like the bland boiled cabbage you might be thinking of. It's sautéed with onions, garlic, bell peppers, carrots, and thyme, then lightly steamed until tender-crisp. Some cooks add a touch of scotch bonnet for a little kick.

This side brings a fresh, slightly sweet crunch to the plate that balances out the richness of dishes like jerk chicken and oxtails. It's one of those sides that people don't expect to love — but then they can't stop eating it.

🥬 Fun Fact: Jamaican steamed cabbage is often seasoned with the same herbs used in the main dishes — thyme, garlic, and peppers — so it ties the whole plate together in perfect harmony!
🍌

Fried Plantains

Plantains are a staple across the Caribbean, West Africa, and Central America. In Jamaica, fried plantains are a beloved side — the trick is using ripe plantains with mostly black skin, meaning the starches have converted to sugar. When sliced and fried, they caramelize outside while staying soft and sweet inside.

Plantains were brought to the Caribbean from West Africa and Southeast Asia and quickly became a cornerstone of island cooking.

🍌 Fun Fact: Plantains are a dietary staple for over 100 million people across Africa, the Caribbean, and Latin America!
🧀

Mac & Cheese

Caribbean mac and cheese is a whole different level from the boxed stuff. Jamaican-style mac and cheese is baked until golden and crispy on top, with a rich, creamy interior loaded with a blend of cheeses. It's denser and more custard-like than American versions, almost like a savory cheesecake made with pasta.

The secret is using evaporated milk and eggs in the cheese mixture, which gives it that firm, sliceable texture when baked. Some cooks add a touch of mustard powder or scotch bonnet pepper for extra flavor. At Irie Cuisine, our mac and cheese is the ultimate creamy, cheesy comfort food side.

🧀 Fun Fact: Caribbean mac and cheese is traditionally baked in the oven, not made on the stovetop — that's what gives it the golden, crispy top layer that everyone fights over!
🥒

Cucumber Salad

Jamaican cucumber salad is the refreshing cool-down your taste buds need alongside spicy Caribbean dishes. Crisp sliced cucumbers are tossed with vinegar, onions, scotch bonnet pepper, and a pinch of salt — creating a tangy, crunchy side that cleanses the palate between bites of rich, bold food.

This simple salad has roots in the Caribbean tradition of serving pickled or vinegar-based sides with heavy meals. The acidity cuts through the richness of oxtails and curried goat, while the cool cucumber brings relief from the heat of scotch bonnet.

🥒 Fun Fact: Jamaican cucumber salad doubles as a natural coolant — the vinegar and cucumber combination is one of the best ways to tame the heat from scotch bonnet peppers!
🧈

Festival

Festival is a sweet, fried Jamaican dumpling — crispy outside, soft and slightly sweet inside. Made from cornmeal, flour, sugar, and salt, shaped into fingers and deep-fried golden brown. The perfect companion to jerk chicken and escovitch fish.

Festival is believed to have originated at Jamaican street food stalls and beach parties — hence the name!

🎶 Fun Fact: Festival gets its name from the festive occasions where it was originally served — beach parties, cookouts, and celebrations. It literally turns every meal into a festival!
